# Feudal Games Comparison

## Overview

This document synthesizes analysis from 18 notable medieval/feudal video games spanning 2015-2026, identifying patterns in mechanics, design approaches, market reception, and opportunities for [[game-design-md|gamedev-feudal]].

## Market Overview

The medieval/feudal game market has seen substantial growth:
- Multiple titles exceeded 1-3 million copies sold
- Early Access is the dominant development model
- Solo and small-team development success stories are prominent ([[Manor Lords]] by one developer, [[Against the Storm]] by a small team)
- Publishers like [[Hooded Horse]] have emerged as medieval/strategy indie specialists

## Mechanical Trends

### Universal Patterns
1. **[[Vassalage systems]]**: Nearly all games incorporate some form of social hierarchy
2. **Land as power**: Territory management is central to the genre
3. **Economic simulation**: Production chains, trade, taxation, and resource management are pervasive
4. **Class systems**: Games increasingly model class distinctions explicitly
5. **Rebellion vs. Reinforcement split**: Some games have players as feudal overlords, others as challengers to feudal power

### Best-in-Class Implementations

| Mechanic | Best Implementation | Why It Works |
|----------|-------------------|--------------|
| Vassal Loyalty | Crusader Kings III (multi-axis opinion/dread/factions) | Named characters with competing interests, not spreadsheets |
| Land Management | Manor Lords (visual organic growth) | Shows settlement growth; players see results |
| Succession | CK3 (preparation = gameplay) | Player agency over outcomes; dynastic perspective |
| Economy | Medieval Dynasty (tactile chains) | Visceral, understandable resource flows |
| Castle Building | First-person/3D placement | Creates ownership: "I placed every wall" |
| Army Management | Named commanders + rank-and-file (Bannerlord, Battle Brothers) | Emotional investment in leaders, strategic scale for masses |

### Common Pitfalls

1. **Single-axis loyalty** systems that players trivially max by gifting
2. **Abstract territory** that only changes numerically, not visually
3. **Succession as punishment** rather than gameplay
4. **End game economy saturation** — gold accumulates with nowhere to spend it
5. **Spreadsheet-only armies** with no named characters
6. **Micromanagement death spirals** from too many individual buildings

## Market Positioning for gamedev-feudal

### White Space / Underserved Areas
- **Feudal politics and relationship management** (most games focus on building or combat, not politics)
- **Casual/educational feudal games** (market skews toward complex strategy or hardcore action)
- **Cooperative feudal gameplay** (very few games let players share lordship)
- **Narrative-driven feudal experiences with ethical design choices**

### Pricing Strategy
- **Sweet spot**: $14.99-$19.99 for indie medieval games
- Above $25 requires exceptional quality or established franchise
- Early access pricing at 20% discount accelerates adoption

### Target Positioning
The project occupies intersection of: educational intent, casual accessibility, feudal politics, and ethical design. No existing game occupies this exact space — [[Crusader Kings III]] is too complex, [[Medieval Dynasty]] is survival-first, [[Manor Lords]] is city-building-focused with minimal feudal relationships.
