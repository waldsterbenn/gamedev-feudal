# Medieval Military System

## Overview

The feudal military system was built on the relationship between land ownership and military service. The [[knight]] was the backbone of feudal military power, but the system included levies, retinues, and eventually professional soldiers.

## The Feudal Levy

- **Host**: The army summoned by a lord from his [[vassal|vassals]]
- **Service limit**: Typically 40 days per year — one campaigning season
- **Problem**: The 40-day limit made extended campaigns and long sieges nearly impossible

## Military Household

As feudal levies proved insufficient:
- **Retinue**: Knights and men-at-arms permanently maintained by a lord
- **Mercenaries**: Increasingly common from the 12th century onward
- **Cost problem**: Mercenaries were expensive; many lords went into debt to maintain forces

## Castle Networks

- **Strategic role**: Castles were administrative centers, not purely military fortifications
- **Control mechanism**: A network of castles controlled territory; each was a seat of local government
- **Economic cost**: Major castles (Dover, Conwy) cost tens of thousands of pounds — massive investments
- **Decline**: Gunpowder artillery rendered castle walls obsolete by the 15th century

## Code of Chivalry

Developed in the 12th-13th centuries, emphasizing:
- Loyalty to lord
- Courage in battle
- Courtesy
- Protection of the weak and of the Church

In practice, chivalric ideals often clashed with the brutal realities of medieval warfare.

## Key Battles and Military Evolution

| Battle | Year | Significance |
|--------|------|-------------|
| Hastings | 1066 | Classic feudal cavalry victory over Anglo-Saxon infantry shield wall |
| Legnano | 1176 | Lombard League infantry defeats Frederick Barbarossa's feudal cavalry |
| Courtrai/Golden Spurs | 1302 | Flemish infantry destroys French knightly army |
| Crecy | 1346 | English longbowmen decimate French chivalry |
| Agincourt | 1415 | English victory confirms professional infantry dominance |
| Formigny | 1450 | Gunpowder artillery renders castles obsolete |

## Game Design Application

Military systems in feudal games should balance:
- **Named characters** for emotional investment vs. **abstract armies** for strategic scale
- Recruitment constraints (time, cost, skill requirements)
- Logistics (supply lines, season, morale)
- Warfare as commander experience: positioning, timing, morale management
- The siege-construction feedback loop

The best implementation combines named commanders with faceless rank-and-file: emotional investment in leaders with strategic scale for the masses.
