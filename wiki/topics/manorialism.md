# Manorialism

## Overview

**Manorialism** was the economic system governing relations between lords and [[peasants]] on the manor. While [[feudalism]] described the political/military relationships between nobles, manorialism described the economic engine that made feudalism possible.

## The Manor as Economic Unit

The manor (or lordship) was the basic economic cell of feudalism, aiming for **self-sufficiency**:

- **Demesne**: The lord's own land, worked by serfs as part of their labor obligation
- **Peasant holdings**: Strips in open fields for subsistence farming
- **Commons**: Shared pasture, woodland, and water sources
- **Infrastructure**: Village church, mill, bakehouse, and blacksmith

## Key Features

### Land Organization

Peasants held scattered strips across open fields (not consolidated plots), ensuring each family got some good and some poor land. This created a communal farming system with shared decision-making about crop rotation and planting schedules.

### Labor Obligations

- **Serfs/villeins**: Required to work the lord's demesne typically 2-3 days per week
- **Additional duties**: Milling at the lord's mill, baking at the lord's oven, marriage fees, death duties
- **Freemen**: Paid rent but could move, marry, and own property

### Economic Outputs

The manor aimed to produce everything it needed internally. Any surplus went to the [[lord]] and could be traded at weekly markets or annual fairs.

## Currency and Trade

- Much of the manorial economy operated on labor obligations and in-kind payments rather than coin
- The silver penny (denier) was the dominant coin for centuries
- By the 13th century, monetization enabled commutation of labor services into cash payments (rent)

## Game Design Application

Manorial mechanics should model:
- Direct vs. indirect land control trade-offs
- Production chains from field to surplus
- Population management and peasant satisfaction
- Surplus/deficit dynamics across seasons

Manor Lords' visual approach to manorialism (burgage plots, organic growth, visible workforce) has been highly praised compared to abstract approaches.
