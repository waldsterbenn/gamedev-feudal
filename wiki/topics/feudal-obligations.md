# Feudal Obligations

## Overview

Feudalism was built on **reciprocal obligations** — every relationship was a two-way contract of service and protection. When obligations were met, the system functioned. When they were broken, conflict ensued.

## Vassal's Obligations

### Military Service
- Typically **40 days per year** of armed service
- This time limit made long sieges and extended campaigns difficult
- Led to the rise of permanent military households and eventually mercenaries

### Counsel and Court Attendance
- Required attendance at the lord's court to advise on matters and participate in judgment
- Reinforced the personal and political nature of feudal bonds

### Financial Aids
Required payments on specific occasions:
- Lord's ransom if captured
- Knighting of the lord's eldest son
- Marriage of the lord's eldest daughter

### Relief
Inheritance tax paid when entering a fief. Under Magna Carta: 100 pounds for a barony.

### Wardship and Marriage Rights
If an heir was a minor, the lord controlled the fief and its revenues until adulthood, and influenced the heir's marriage.

## Lord's Obligations

### Protection
Military defense of the vassal's lands against external threats.

### Justice
Fair adjudication of disputes in the lord's court. In practice, the lord judged his own tenants — though higher justice (life and death) was sometimes reserved for the king.

### Maintenance
Ensuring the vassal could sustain their station — this was the fundamental justification for granting the [[fief]].

## Breaking Obligations

When lords failed their obligations (unjust seizure, excessive demands, failure to protect), it created the conditions for:
- **Rebellion** by [[vassal|vassals]] — the most common threat to any lord
- **Appeal to higher authority** (e.g., barons forcing King John to sign Magna Carta)
- **Defection** to rival lords

## Game Design Application

Obligation systems should track:
- What each vassal owes (military, financial, counsel)
- What the lord owes each vassal (protection, justice, maintenance)
- Consequences of unmet obligations on both sides
- Cascading failures across chains of obligation

The tension between competing demands — some vassals want lower taxes, others want military campaigns, the church wants donations — creates the compelling political drama of feudalism.
