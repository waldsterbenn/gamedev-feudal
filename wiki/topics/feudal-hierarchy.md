# Feudal Hierarchy

## Overview

The feudal hierarchy was a strictly stratified social structure with obligations flowing both downward and upward. At its theoretical apex stood the [[king]], with land granted progressively downward through layers of [[vassal|vassals]].

## Hierarchy Structure

```
                        KING (Suzerain/Monarch)
                                   │ grants fiefs
           ┌───────────────────────┼───────────────────────┐
           ▼                       ▼                       ▼
      DUKE/PRINCE           ARCHBISHOP/POPE           EARL/COUNT
      (grants to:           (grants to:               (grants to:
           ▼                       ▼                       ▼
      BARON/TENANT             KNIGHT (Miles)         LESSER NOBLE
                               (grants to:
                                    ▼
                          FREEMAN PEASANT
                          SERF / VILLEIN
                          (Bonded to the land)
                               │
                          SLAVE (early period, phased out by ~12th c.)
```

## Key Ranks

### [[king]]
Supreme landowner in theory. All land ultimately held "in chief" from the Crown. Powers included granting fiefs, summoning armies, dispensing justice, minting coins, and levying taxes. In practice, constrained by powerful [[vassal|vassals]] as demonstrated by Magna Carta (1215).

### [[duke]]
Ruled large territories with multiple counties. Near-sovereign within their domain; maintained private armies, dispensed justice, collected taxes. Example: William of Normandy commanded more resources than the King of France.

### [[count]] / Earl
Administered counties/shires as regional governors and military leaders. Collected taxes, administered courts, kept portion of fines and tolls.

### [[baron]]
Held land directly from the king (tenant-in-chief) or from higher nobles. Controlled castles and surrounding territory. Military obligation: typically provide set number of knights (40 days/year).

### [[knight]]
Professional mounted warrior — the backbone of feudal military power. Required expensive equipment (destrier warhorse, chain mail/plate armor, sword, lance, shield) and supporting land (knight's fee). The [[code of chivalry]] developed in 12th-13th centuries.

### [[clergy]] (Parallel Hierarchy)
Pope → Archbishop/Bishop → Abbot/Abbess → Parish Priest. The Church operated as a massive feudal power alongside the secular hierarchy.

### [[peasants]]
Freemen (legally free) and serfs/villeins (bound to land). Made up 60-90% of the population.

## Sub-Infeudation

A critical feature: vassals could themselves grant portions of their land to sub-vassals, creating cascading chains of obligation. This made the hierarchy complex and often conflicting — a vassal's vassal was not necessarily the lord's direct concern.

## Game Design Application

Understanding the hierarchy is essential for modeling:
- Power dynamics and who can challenge whom
- Military logistics (who provides how many troops)
- Chain of command for issuing orders
- Sources of rebellion at different levels
- Succession crises at each rank
