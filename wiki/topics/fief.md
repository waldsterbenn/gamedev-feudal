# Fief (Feudum)

## Overview

The **fief** (Latin: *feudum*) was the fundamental unit of [[feudalism]]. A fief was a grant of land — or rights to collect revenue from land — given by a lord to a [[vassal]].

## Characteristics

- **Size variation**: Ranged from a single manor/village to an entire duchy or kingdom
- **Income generation**: The fief provided revenue through [[peasant]] labor, taxes, tolls, milling rights, and justice fees
- **Hereditary tenure**: Over time, fiefs became hereditary possessions, typically passing from father to eldest son through **primogeniture**
- **Economic basis**: Required supporting land (e.g., a knight's fee) to sustain the vassal's station and obligations

## Types of Fiefs

- **Barony**: Held directly from the [[king]] (tenant-in-chief); typically included a castle and surrounding territory
- **Knight's fee**: Portion of land sufficient to support one [[knight]] and their equipment
- **Duchy**: Large territories with multiple counties, held by a [[duke]]
- **Ecclesiastical fiefs**: Church lands granted to [[clergy]] members; the Church controlled 20-30% of all productive land in some regions

## Income Sources from a Fief

- Peasant labor on the lord's **demesne** (home farm)
- Taxes and tolls
- Milling and baking monopolies
- Justice fees from manorial courts
- Weekly markets and annual fairs

## Inheritance and Transfer

Upon a vassal's death, the heir paid a **relief** (inheritance tax) to enter the fief. Under Magna Carta, a barony's relief was set at 100 pounds — a significant sum that could be ruinous. If the heir was a minor, the lord claimed **wardship** — control of the fief and its revenues until adulthood.

## Game Design Application

For games modeling feudalism, fiefs represent the primary resource and win condition. The tension between keeping land for direct income (**demesne management**) versus granting it to [[vassal|vassals]] for military support and political stability is a core strategic decision. See [[demense management]] and [[feudal obligations]] for related mechanics.
