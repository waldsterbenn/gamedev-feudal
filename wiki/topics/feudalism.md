# Feudalism

## Overview

Feudalism was a socio-political-economic system based on the holding of land (**[[fief]]**) in exchange for service or labor. It emerged in medieval Europe as a response to the collapse of centralized authority following the Fall of Rome (476 CE) and became the dominant organizing principle of society for roughly 600 years (approximately 9th to 15th centuries).

**Core principle**: Land is the ultimate source of power and wealth. Everything flows from who controls the land, and reciprocal obligations bind every level of society together.

## Three Essential Pillars

1. **Land (fief)** - All power derives from land ownership
2. **Reciprocal obligation** - Every relationship is a two-way contract of service and protection
3. **Hierarchy** - Society is strictly stratified, with obligations flowing downward and upward

## Related Systems

Feudalism is often confused with [[manorialism]], but they are distinct:
- **Feudalism**: The political and military relationship between nobles (lord-vassal)
- **Manorialism**: The economic system governing relations between lords and [[peasants]] on the manor

Manorialism was the economic engine; feudalism was the political superstructure.

## Feudal Hierarchy

The [[feudal hierarchy]] placed the [[king]] at the apex, with land granted downward through [[duke]]s, [[count|count]]s, [[baron]]s, and [[knight]]s, down to the peasantry. Parallel to this was the [[clergy]] hierarchy led by the Pope and including archbishops, bishops, and abbots who controlled vast estates.

## Regional Variations

Feudalism manifested differently across regions:
- **England**: Most "pure" feudal system after 1066 Norman Conquest; all land technically belonged to the Crown
- **France**: Classic but fragmented; royal power extremely weak in 9th-10th centuries
- **Holy Roman Empire**: Feudal power fragmented into competing principalities with electoral emperor selection
- **Japan**: Parallel development with [[daimyo]] and [[samurai]] analogous to European dukes and knights

Non-European feudal systems include the Islamic **Iqta**, Ottoman **Timar**, and Zhou Dynasty **Fēngjiàn**.

## Timeline Summary

| Period | Dates | Key Development |
|--------|-------|-----------------|
| Fall of Rome | 476 CE | Collapse of centralized imperial authority |
| Carolingian Origins | 700s-800s | Charlemagne formalizes vassalage |
| Classic Feudalism | 1000-1200 | System at its peak |
| Decline | 1347-1500 | Black Death, professional armies, rise of nation-states |

## Game Design Implications

For [[feudal game design]], feudalism provides core systemic elements: land as power, [[vassal loyalty]] dynamics, obligation webs that can break, succession crises as natural conflict points, and religious authority as ally/rival/threat.

## Notes

- The traditional feudal model has been critiqued by revisionist historians (notably Susan Reynolds, *Fiefs and Vassals*, 1994) who argue the term "feudalism" itself is a later construct
- Despite academic debate, the model remains useful for understanding medieval social organization
- Adaptation for gameplay balance is recommended over strict historical accuracy
