# Three Estates (Ordo)

## Overview

Medieval society was theorized as three mutually supportive orders, a model described by **Bishop Adalbero of Laon** around 1020 CE. This "three orders" model provided ideological justification for the entire feudal hierarchy.

## The Three Orders

### 1. Those Who Pray (Oratores)

The **[[clergy]]** — responsible for spiritual salvation of all. This included the Pope, archbishops, bishops, abbots, abbesses, and parish priests. The Church was the largest single landowner in medieval Europe, controlling 20-30% of all productive land in some regions.

The clergy had a parallel [[feudal hierarchy]]: Pope → Archbishop/Bishop → Abbot/Abbess → Parish Priest.

### 2. Those Who Fight (Bellatores)

The **nobility and military** — responsible for physical protection. This included the [[king]], [[duke]]s, [[count|count]]s, [[baron]]s, and [[knight]]s. They justified their privileged position through the provision of military defense and justice.

### 3. Those Who Work (Laboratores)

The **[[peasants]] and serfs** — responsible for material sustenance. Making up 60-90% of the population, they included freemen (legally free but economically dependent), serfs/villeins (bound to the land), and slaves (early period, phased out by ~12th century).

## Social Function

The three estates model portrayed a mutually supportive system where each order needed the others:
- The clergy saved souls
- The nobility provided protection
- The peasants fed everyone

In reality, this ideology served to justify inequality and discourage social mobility by framing the hierarchy as divinely ordained.

## Tensions and Reality

The three estates model was an idealized construct. Tensions included:
- **Church vs. Crown**: Investiture conflicts over who appointed bishops
- **Noble rivalries**: Higher and lower nobles frequently in conflict
- **Peasant uprisings**: Exploitation led to revolts (Wat Tyler 1381, Jacquerie 1358)
- **Rising towns**: Merchant class (burgers/burghers) existed outside the feudal system entirely

## Game Design Application

Class distinction is an increasingly common mechanic in feudal games:
- **Pentiment** explicitly explores class dynamics and social tensions
- **Kingdom Come: Deliverance** forces the player to climb from commoner through social ranks
- **Against the Storm** uses tiered worker classes with different needs
- **CK3** models multi-tiered noble hierarchies with different privileges
