# King

## Overview

The [[king]] was the supreme landowner in feudal theory, though in practice often constrained by powerful [[vassal|vassals]]. All land was ultimately held "in chief" from the crown.

## Role and Powers

- Grant [[fief|fiefs]] to [[vassal|vassals]]
- Summon armies through feudal levies
- Dispense highest justice
- Mint coins
- Levy certain taxes
- Receive feudal aids and reliefs

## Revenue Sources

- Crown lands (direct demesne)
- Feudal aids (payments on special occasions)
- Reliefs (inheritance tax on fiefs — 100 pounds for a barony under Magna Carta)
- Scutage (payments in lieu of military service)
- Various taxes and tolls

## Historical Reality

Medieval kings were far from absolute. **King John of England** was forced to sign **Magna Carta (1215)** precisely because his barons had the military power to demand limits on royal authority. Similarly, the [[king|King of France]] was technically the feudal lord of the [[Duke of Normandy]], but the Duke often controlled far more territory and wealth.

## English Uniqueness

The English system was unique in its centralization:
- **William the Conqueror** (1066) kept ~20% of land under direct crown control — more than any continental ruler
- **Salisbury Oath (1086)**: All major landowners swore direct allegiance to the king
- **Domesday Book (1086)**: Complete land survey of 12,400+ settlements
- **Sheriff system**: Royal officials enforced the king's writ at county level

## Game Design Application

In feudal games, the king represents either:
- **The player** (when playing as a [[king]]) — managing the tension between central power and [[vassal]] autonomy
- **An external authority** (when playing as a lower lord) — a source of obligations, potential protection, and occasional threat
- **An AI ruler** — whose decisions and personality shape the political landscape
