# Knight (Miles)

## Overview

The [[knight]] (Old English: *cniht* — servant, follower; Latin: *miles* — soldier) was a professional mounted warrior and the backbone of feudal military power. Knights required substantial supporting land (a knight's fee) to maintain the expensive equipment required for warfare.

## Equipment

Knights' equipment was extremely expensive and included:
- **Destrier**: War horse, carefully bred and trained
- **Armor**: Chain mail (early period), later plate armor
- **Weapons**: Sword, lance, shield
- **Support**: Squires, grooms, and servants

## Training and Status

- Training began in youth (as a page, then squire)
- Required years of martial training
- Achieved knighthood through a ceremonial dubbing
- The [[Code of Chivalry]] (12th-13th centuries) emphasized loyalty, courage, courtesy, and protection of the weak and the Church

## Economic Basis

A knight required a **knight's fee** — a portion of land sufficient to support the cost of equipment, training, and household. This tied military capability directly to the [[fief]] system.

## Code of Chivalry

Developed in the 12th-13th centuries, emphasizing:
- Loyalty to [[lord]]
- Courage in battle
- Courtesy and good manners
- Protection of the weak
- Service to the [[clergy]] and the Church

In practice, these ideals often clashed with the brutal realities of medieval warfare, siege aftermaths, and political intrigue.

## Military Evolution

Knights dominated battlefields until a series of defeats showed the vulnerability of heavy cavalry:
- **Legnano (1176)**: Lombard infantry defeats Frederick Barbarossa
- **Courtrai (1302)**: Flemish infantry destroys French knights
- **Crecy (1346)**: English longbowmen decimate French chivalry
- **Agincourt (1415)**: Professional infantry confirms dominance

## Game Design Application

Knights are natural candidates for:
- Named characters with personalities, ambitions, and relationships
- Key military commanders with unique abilities
- Court positions and council members
- Characters players can recruit, promote, or betray

Players form the strongest attachments to named characters, so knights are among the most valuable character types for emotional investment.
