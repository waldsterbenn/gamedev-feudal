# Vassal

## Overview

A vassal was a person who entered into a mutual obligation relationship with a [[lord]] through the ceremony of [[homage]] and oath of [[fealty]]. In exchange for receiving a [[fief]] (land grant), the vassal owed military service, counsel, and financial aids to their lord.

## Types of Vassals

- **Tenants-in-chief**: Held land directly from the [[king]] ([[baron|barons]], [[duke|dukes]], [[count|counts]])
- **Sub-vassals**: Held land from a higher [[vassal]] ([[knight|knights]], lesser nobles)
- **Ecclesiastical vassals**: [[Clergy]] holding land as vassals (bishops, abbots)

## Key Concepts

### Multiple Vassalage
A vassal could hold [[fief|fiefs]] from multiple lords, creating conflicting loyalties. This was a major structural weakness of feudalism.

### Sub-Infeudation
Vassals could themselves grant portions of their land to sub-vassals, creating cascading chains of obligation.

## Vassal Obligations to Lord

1. **Military service**: Typically 40 days per year
2. **Counsel**: Attendance at lord's court
3. **Financial aids**: Lord's ransom, knighting of eldest son, marriage of eldest daughter
4. **Relief**: Inheritance tax upon entering a fief
5. **Wardship**: If heir was minor, lord controlled the fief

## Lord's Obligations to Vassal

1. **Protection**: Military defense
2. **Justice**: Fair adjudication of disputes
3. **Maintenance**: Ensuring the vassal could sustain their station

## Game Design Application

[[Vassal loyalty systems]] should model:
- Named vassals with personalities, ambitions, and inter-relationships
- Multiple loyalty axes (opinion, obligation satisfaction, ambition)
- Faction formation around shared grievances
- Rebellion as dramatic story generation
