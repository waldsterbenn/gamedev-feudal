## 1. Medieval & Feudal Video Games Survey

This survey catalogs 18 notable video games set in medieval or feudal-themed worlds, spanning 2015-2026 (with a few foundational pre-2015 titles included for context where their modern editions or influence is significant). Each entry documents release year, developer, genre, key feudal mechanics, commercial performance, and critical reception.

#### 1. Kingdom Come: Deliverance (2018)

- **Developer:** Warhorse Studios
- **Publisher:** Deep Silver
- **Genre:** Action RPG, Open World
- **Release Date:** February 2018 (PC, PS4, Xbox One)

### Key Patterns and Observations

**Mechanical Trends:**
1. **Vassalage & Hierarchy** — Nearly all medieval games incorporate some form of social hierarchy, from CK3's complex feudal obligation chains to Kingdom Two Crowns' minimalist monarch-subject relationship.
2. **Land as Power** — Territory and land management are central: Manor Lords' burgage plots, Bannerlord's fiefdom system, Medieval Dynasty's settlement founding, Against the Storm's multi-settlement model.
3. **Economic Simulation** — Production chains, trade networks, taxation, and resource management appear across the genre, reflecting the agricultural and mercantile basis of medieval feudalism.
4. **Class Systems** — Games increasingly model class distinctions explicitly: Pentiment's focus on social strata, KCD's commoner-to-noble progression, CK3's multi-tiered noble hierarchy.
5. **Rebellion vs. Reinforcement** — A notable split: some games have players as feudal overlords (CK3, Manor Lords, Medieval Dynasty), while others cast them as challengers to feudal power (Bellwright, Wartales, Battle Brothers, Pentiment).

**Genre Distribution:**
- Grand Strategy: 3 (CK3, Songs of Conquest, Ancestors Legacy)
- Tactical RPG: 4 (Wartales, Battle Brothers, King Arthur, Pentiment)
- City Builder / Colony Sim: 5 (Manor Lords, Medieval Dynasty, Going Medieval, Farthest Frontier, Against the Storm)
- Action RPG / Open World: 3 (KCD 1 & 2, Bellwright)
- Strategy / Hybrid: 3 (Bannerlord, Kingdom Two Crowns, Beloved Rapture)

**Commercial Trends (2020-2026):**
- The medieval/feudal game market has seen substantial growth, with several titles exceeding 1-3 million copies sold.
- Early Access as a development model is dominant (Manor Lords, Bannerlord, Medieval Dynasty, Going Medieval, Bellwright, Against the Storm, Farthest Frontier).
- Solo and small-team development success stories are prominent: Manor Lords (1 person), Against the Storm (small team), Medieval Dynasty (Render Cube, ~15 people).
- Publishers like Hooded Horse have emerged as specialists in medieval/strategy indie games.

**Critical Consensus:**
- Games praised for historical authenticity (KCD, Pentiment), deep systemic feudal simulation (CK3), and innovative mechanical integration (Against the Storm, Manor Lords) receive the highest critical scores.
- Early Access games often see significant score improvements post-launch.
- Multiplayer combat-focused titles (Chivalry 2, Mordhau) were excluded from this detailed survey as they lack feudal simulation mechanics despite their medieval settings.


## 2. Game Design Mechanics Analysis

### 2.1 Vassal Loyalty and Rebellion Systems

The way feudal games model vassal loyalty is the single most defining design choice in the genre.

**Best Implementation: Crusader Kings III**
- Multi-layered model: Base opinion (personality, events, interactions) + feudal obligation (contract terms: levies/taxes) + dread (authority through fear) + faction system (organized resistance) + vassal power (de jure/demesne limits)
- Vassals don't just have a loyalty number -- they have ambitions, relationships with other vassals, opinions of the ruler's court positions, religion, culture, and more
- Rebellion is not just punitive -- it generates emergent narrative: "My brother-in-law the Duke of Aquitaine allied with my rival the Count of Flanders and demanded my wife become the new King"

**Simpler Model: Bannerlord**
- Single-axis relationship system (opinion +100 to -100)
- Vassals may defect to other kingdoms if relations are poor
- Lacks the depth of CK3 but makes the system readable and predictable
- Problem: Too mechanical -- vassals feel like NPCs with opinion bars, not people

**What Works:**
- Named vassals with personalities, ambitions, and relationships with each other (not just with the player)
- Multiple axes of loyalty: opinion, obligation satisfaction, fear/respect, shared interests
- Rebellion as dramatic event generating stories, not just a checkbox failure state
- Factions that group vassals around shared grievances (taxes, religion, claims)

**What Fails:**
- Single metric loyalty that players can trivially max out by giving gifts
- Vassals who only exist as revenue/troop generators with no internal logic
- Rebellion without narrative framing -- it feels like punishment rather than drama
- No consequences for "perfect" rule (every player should face tension between competing demands)

**Key Insight:** The most engaging feudal games make vassals feel like characters with competing interests, not spreadsheets with attitude bars. Players remember stories: "My most loyal vassal betrayed me because I promoted a rival's son to court."

### 2.2 Land and Territory Management

**Spatial abstraction levels (from abstract to tangible):**
- **Abstract** (CK3): Land is a colored shape on a political map with modifiers. You manage tax income and levies from each holding.
- **Visual/organic** (Manor Lords): Land is a living space you can walk through. Burgage plots grow organically along roads. You see peasants living and working.
- **Vertical construction** (Going Medieval): Land is a 3D fortress you build from the ground up, with layers and rooms.
- **Frontier survival** (Medieval Dynasty): Land is wilderness you clear, build upon, and bring settlers to.

**What Works:**
- Meaningful tension between demesne (direct control) and vassal land (indirect control). CK3's demesne limit forces this trade-off: keep land for personal income vs. grant it to vassals for levies and political stability.
- Land feels alive when you can see it -- Manor Lords' success is partly because you watch your settlement grow from above
- Production chains that connect geographically (Manor Lords, Against the Storm's trade hubs)
- The "zoom out" effect: players love seeing the macro picture of their domain grow

**What Fails:**
- Micromanagement death spiral: too many individual buildings/villages to manage directly
- Static maps where territory changes only numerically, not visually
- Land grants that feel like losing territory rather than gaining assets (vassals should clearly benefit the lord)
- No meaningful difference between types of terrain/land (all provinces equivalent)

**Key Insight:** The most successful games give players both the granular view (this farm feeds 12 people) and the grand view (my kingdom now spans three provinces). The tension between detail and scope is where the fun lives.

### 2.3 Succession and Inheritance Mechanics

**What Makes It Good (CK3's Gold Standard):**
- Succession is the strategic game, not a penalty. Partition laws create genuine decisions: which children to prepare, which lands to arrange
- Multiple succession types (partition, primogeniture, elective, gavelkind, etc.) each create different gameplay experiences
- Dynastic perspective: you play a bloodline, not a person. Individual death is expected and often interesting

**What Makes It Bad:**
- Succession as frustrating interruption (game stops, everything resets)
- No player agency over the outcome
- Only relevant in early game; late-game players have enough power to neutralize succession threats

**Key Insight:** Succession mechanics are only compelling when the player can influence them. CK3 works because you spend your entire reign preparing for succession -- marrying well, assigning guardianship, managing claims, and positioning heirs. When succession is something that happens *to* you, it's just a chore.

### 2.4 Economy and Resource Chains

**Two Approaches:**

**Tactile/survival (Medieval Dynasty, Against the Storm):**
- You gather wood, stone, food; process them; build; trade
- Resource chains are visceral and understandable
- Creates satisfying gameplay loops but can become repetitive

**Abstract/strategic (CK3, Bannerlord):**
- Gold is the universal resource
- Income from taxes, trade, domain revenue
- Strategic depth but less satisfying "feel"

**What Works:**
- Multiple income paths enable different strategies (taxes vs. trade vs. production)
- Scarcity creates creativity; abundance kills engagement
- Trade between settlements/towns creates geopolitical dynamics
- Player can see the economy grow organically

**Common Failure:**
- Late-game economy saturation: once you have "enough," the challenge vanishes. This is the #1 shared failure mode across the entire genre.
- Inflation: gold becomes meaningless; players accumulate millions with nothing to spend on
- No resource sinks that feel meaningful

**Key Insight:** The economy should scale with the player's domain size. Small domain = constant scarcity tension. Large domain = different problems (managing surplus, avoiding waste, investing in prestige projects).

### 2.5 Castle Building and Castle Network Systems

The castle-as-capital-of-region is central to the feudal lord fantasy.

**Direct Construction (Medieval Dynasty, Going Medieval):**
- Players build structures piece by piece
- Creates ownership and attachment
- "I placed every wall, I laid every beam"
- Problem: Time-consuming; can slow pacing

**Abstract Construction (CK3, Bannerlord):**
- "Upgrade village to town" with a progress bar
- Fast but unsatisfying
- No visual connection between player effort and result

**The Middle Ground (Manor Lords):**
- You place the building but with organic placement rules
- Building grows and develops over time
- Visual feedback on the result

**What Works for Castles Specifically:**
- Defensive testing: the moment a raid or siege proves your design choices matter
- Regional castle networks: each castle serves a purpose (border defense, trade hub, administrative center)
- Castle upgrades that feel progressive and earned
- The siege-construction feedback loop: you build, you test, you improve

**Key Insight:** Castle building is most engaging when it has stakes -- when there's a credible threat that will test your defenses, and when castle placement has strategic meaning on the map.

---

### 2.6 Army Recruitment and Military Systems

**Named Soldiers (Dwarf Fortress's Gold Standard):**
- Every soldier has a name, history, skills, personality
- Losing a veteran soldier hurts emotionally
- This is the single most powerful player-investment mechanic

**Abstract Armies (CK3, Total War):**
- Armies are numbers on a map
- Strategic scale but emotional emptiness
- "I lost 5000 men" vs. "I lost Sir Gareth of Winchester"

**The Middle (Bannerlord, Battle Brothers):**
- Named companions and core squad, faceless rank-and-file
- Best of both worlds: emotional investment in leaders, strategic scale for the masses

**What Works:**
- Recruitment constraints (time, cost, skill requirements) enable real strategic decisions
- Warfare as commander experience: positioning, timing, morale management
- Army logistics matter: supply lines, season, morale
- Military victories feel earned through preparation and tactics, not just bigger numbers

**What Fails:**
- Combat as spreadsheet calculation (auto-resolve only)
- Armies that spawn instantly from nothing
- No consequences for military defeat (just restart and try again)

**Key Insight:** Players form the strongest attachment to named characters. Even in a grand-scale game, naming the core leaders of your armies (generals, knights, commanders) creates emotional investment that number-only armies cannot match.

### 2.7 Player Psychology: The 'Lord Fantasy'

**Core Psychological Drivers:**
1. **Control** -- shaping a domain, directing people, building from nothing
2. **Growth** -- watching your settlement/fiefdom expand and prosper
3. **Social connection** -- relationships with vassals, allies, rivals, family
4. **Competence** -- mastering complex systems and solving problems
5. **Identity** -- "I am a lord shaping the fate of a kingdom" -- role-identification

**What Players Respond To:**
- Character-driven systems: Named NPCs with stories > abstract mechanics
- Emergent narrative: "I didn't plan this, it just happened" moments are the most memorable
- Meaningful failure: When things go wrong, players want new opportunities, not punishment
- Tangible progress: Visible growth of their domain, not just numbers going up
- Choice and consequence: Decisions that ripple through the game world

**What Players Resent:**
- System overload: Too many interconnected mechanics that are hard to understand
- Endgame stagnation: The game stops presenting meaningful challenges
- Artificial complexity: Complexity that doesn't serve the experience (UI menus for the sake of menus)
- Ludonarrative dissonance: The game says you're a great lord, but all you do is count grain

**Key Insight:** The feudal lord fantasy works because players want to feel powerful but constrained. Unlimited power is boring. The tension between "I am a lord" and "my powerful vassals have their own agendas" is the emotional core of the genre. Balance the fantasy of control with the reality of distributed power.

|----------|-------------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Vassal Loyalty | CK3 (multi-axis opinion/dread/factions) | Single bar maxed by gifting | Players remember character stories, not loyalty numbers |
| Land Management | Manor Lords (visual growth) | Abstract colored provinces | Visual growth > numerical growth |
| Succession | CK3 (preparation = gameplay) | Frustrating interruption | Only good when player has agency |
| Economy | Medieval Dynasty (tactile chains) | Abstract gold accumulation | Resource chains create engagement loops |
| Castle Building | First-person/3D placement | Abstract upgrades | Placement creates ownership |
| Army Management | Named commanders (Dwarf Fortress) | Spreadsheet armies | Named characters drive emotional investment |

---

### 2.9 Key Takeaways for Feudal Game Design

1. **Narrative emergence is the primary value driver** - Systems should generate stories automatically, not just process numbers
2. **Character matters more than abstraction** - Named vassals, soldiers, and characters are worth the development cost
3. **Failure should redirect, not punish** - A lost battle or rebellious vassal should create a new chapter, not a game over
4. **The scale paradox** - Players want both granular control and grand overview; offer meaningful zooms/layers
5. **Resist perfect optimization** - Feudalism was messy, contradictory, and human. Clean game design should embrace some of that messy humanity
6. **Investment grows through time and naming** - The longer a character exists in the player's world, the more they care when something happens to them
7. **Economy must matter at all stages** - Scarcity in early game, meaningful investment choices in late game

- **#1 review killer:** Early access abandonment without consistent updates
- **Best-reviewed games share:** Regular content updates, responsive developer communication, clear roadmaps

- **Manor Lords** hit "Very Positive" during EA despite being only partially complete

Most effective tag combinations for medieval/feudal games:
- "Medieval" + "City Builder" + "Management" + "Simulation" + "Colony Sim"
- "Grand Strategy" + "Medieval" + "RPG" + "Diplomacy" (CK3 niche)
- "Open World" + "Medieval" + "RPG" + "Action" (KCD niche)
- "Strategy" + "Tactical RPG" + "Medieval" + "Turn-Based" (Battle Brothers niche)

**Emerging niche tag:** "Kingdom Management" - growing in usage, not yet saturated.

### 3.4 Wishlist Strategy and Launch Data

- **Minimum threshold:** 10,000 wishlists for viable Steam launch
- **Target:** 25,000-50,000+ for strong algorithm push
- **Steam Next Fest is the #1 wishlist generator:** Manor Lords gained 1M+ wishlists through Next Fest appearances
- **Conversion rate:** 30-50% of wishlists convert to sales at launch
- **Timeline:** 4-6 months of pre-launch marketing is standard for the genre

### 3.5 Market Saturation Assessment

**Saturated areas:**
- Generic medieval city builders (many similar entries on Steam)
- Survival-craft medieval games (Valheim created a gold rush)
- Low-quality tactical RPGs

**White space / underserved:**
- Feudal politics and relationship management (most games focus on building or combat, not politics)
- Casual/educational feudal games (the market skews toward complex strategy or hardcore action; gentle, educational approach is underrepresented)
- Cooperative feudal gameplay (very few games let players share lordship)
- Narrative-driven feudal experiences with ethical design choices

**Conclusion:** The medieval game market is **moderately** established but not saturated in the niche of feudal social simulation and relationship-based gameplay. Manor Lords proved the audience exists and will spend. A casual, educational approach to feudalism has genuine market whitespace.

### 3.6 Success Case Studies

**Manor Lords (Solo developer, $60M+ week one):**
- What worked: Incredible visual fidelity, deep but accessible city building, authentic medieval feel, aggressive community transparency
- Key lesson: Single developer with a clear vision + exceptional quality in one core area > mediocre attempt at everything

**Against the Storm (Small team, 500K+ sales, Overwhelmingly Positive):**
- What worked: Unique roguelite + colony builder hybrid, short replayable runs, constant updates during EA
- Key lesson: Innovation in mechanical combination creates defensibility against competitors

**Medieval Dynasty (Render Cube, 1.5M+ during EA):**
- What worked: Relaxing medieval life simulation, first-person immersion, dynasty/lineage hook
- Key lesson: The "medieval life" fantasy is underserved; players want to live in a medieval world, not just manage it abstractly

## 4. Key Takeaways for gamedev-feudal Project

### 4.1 Positioning

The project is uniquely positioned at the intersection of:
- **Educational intent** (teaching feudalism through systems)
- **Casual accessibility** (no grind, player-controlled pacing)
- **Feudal politics** (an underserved niche compared to castle builders and combat games)
- **Ethical design** (explicit rejection of dark patterns)

No existing game occupies this exact space. CK3 is too complex for casual players. Medieval Dynasty is survival-first, politics-second. Manor Lords is all about city building with minimal feudal relationships.

### 4.2 Recommended Core Loop

1. **Observe** - View your domain and its needs (harvest season, vassal tensions, trade opportunities)
2. **Decide** - Issue orders, mediate disputes, assign roles, respond to events
3. **Watch** - See consequences play out; vassals react; your domain evolves
4. **Adapt** - New situations emerge; adjust strategy; prepare for succession

This loop avoids repetition through emergent events and character-driven stories, not grinding resource collection.

### 4.3 Mechanics to Prioritize

| Priority | Mechanic | Rationale |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| **P0** | Vassal loyalty/rebel system | Core feudal tension; generates emergent narrative |
| **P0** | Land/demesne management | Land = power is the fundamental feudal principle |
| **P1** | Succession & inheritance | Creates natural story arcs across generations |
| **P1** | Economy (simplified, meaningful chains) | Must matter at all stages, not just early game |
| **P2** | Court and council system | Where the feudal drama plays out |
| **P2** | Faction system | Groups vassals around shared interests |
| **P3** | Military/defense | Important but secondary to social dynamics |
| **P3** | Religion/church relations | Adds depth but can be complex to implement |

### 4.4 Mechanics to Avoid or Simplify

- Complex production chains (go Against the Storm: short, meaningful chains)
- Micromanagement of individual buildings (Manor Lords shows organic placement works better)
- Spreadsheet-only army combat (name your commanders at minimum)
- Endless resource accumulation (add meaningful sinks: prestige projects, castle upgrades, court events)
- Single-axis loyalty systems (use at least 2-3 factors: opinion, obligation, fear/respect)

### 4.5 Market Strategy

- **Price point:** $14.99-$19.99 for early access
- **Platform:** Steam primary, itch.io for prototyping/testing
- **Launch strategy:** 2-3 Steam Next Fest appearances, content creator outreach to 50+ medieval/strategy YouTubers
- **Tags:** "Medieval" + "Management" + "Simulation" + "Strategy" + "Educational" + "Relaxing"
- **Target wishlist:** 10,000 minimum, 25,000+ target before full launch
- **Differentiator:** "Learn feudalism by playing as a feudal lord -- no grind, no dark patterns, no pay-to-win"

### 4.6 Scope Warning

The #1 lesson from this research: **feudal games fail when they try to do everything**. CK3 took decades of development (CK1 + CK2 legacy). Bannerlord spent 10 years in development. Manor Lords is still early access after years of solo work.

Start with a vertical slice: one domain, 5-10 vassals, basic economy, one succession cycle, one or two major events. Get that working and fun before expanding.

### 4.7 Inspirations to Study Closely

| Game | What to Steal | What to Avoid |
|------|--------------|---------------|
| **Crusader Kings III** | Vassal personality system, faction dynamics, succession as gameplay | Overwhelming complexity, DLC-heavy model |
| **Against the Storm** | Roguelite structure creating replayability, short meaningful runs | Abstracted characters (race-based workers with no names) |
| **Manor Lords** | Visual city growth, organic placement, community transparency | Minimal feudal relationship simulation |
| **Medieval Dynasty** | First-person immersion, dynasty mechanics, tactile life simulation | Repetitive survival grind |
| **Pentiment** | Class dynamics, narrative depth, authentic social depiction | No gameplay simulation (pure narrative) |
| **Bannerlord** | Fiefdom management, companion system, tactical combat | Single-axis relationship system |
