# Interview Questionnaire Facilitator Guide

> **Purpose:** How to run effective design interviews using the game development questionnaires.

---

## How to Use These Questionnaires

Each questionnaire is a structured interview guide, not a test. The goal is to extract creative direction and align the team around a shared vision.

### Before the Session

1. **Send the questionnaire to participants 48 hours in advance** so they can think about answers.
2. **Pick 2-3 reference games** to play or watch gameplay of before the session.
3. **Designate a facilitator** (runs the session) and a note-taker (records answers).
4. **Set a time limit** and stick to it. These are comprehensive — you don't need to cover everything in one session.

### During the Session

1. **Work through sections in order** — each builds on the last.
2. **Don't rush through questions.** 2-3 minutes per question is normal for deep topics.
3. **Use follow-up prompts:**
   - "Walk me through an example."
   - "What would the player see/feel in that moment?"
   - "How does that connect to [pillar/feature/lore element]?"
4. **Capture ideas that come up even if they're off-topic.** Park them in a "Parking Lot" section for follow-up.
5. **When opinions differ, don't decide on the spot.** Note the disagreement and schedule a decision meeting.

### After the Session

1. **Summarize key decisions** and distribute them within 24 hours.
2. **Update the relevant design docs** (game-design.md, world-lore.md) with agreed-upon answers.
3. **Create actionable tasks** from open questions (prototypes, research, decisions needed).
4. **Schedule a follow-up** for incomplete sections or new questions that emerged.

---

## Recommended Session Order

1. **Game Design first** (iq-game-design.md) — establishes the gameplay foundation
2. **Lore & World second** (iq-lore-world.md) — builds the world to serve the gameplay
3. **Revisit both** after you have alignment — fill in gaps and resolve conflicts

The reason: lore should serve the mechanics, not the other way around. Knowing what the player DOES first makes world-building more purposeful and avoids creating beautiful lore that has no gameplay function.
